# Financial Analysis: Walmart Inc. (WMT)

This analysis covers five full fiscal years (FY2022–FY2026, each ending January 31) and twenty quarters of quarterly data (Q1 FY2022 through Q4 FY2026). Over the window, Walmart demonstrates a consistent top-line expansion trajectory interrupted by a notable FY2023 profitability trough, followed by a multi-year recovery that has pushed earnings and return metrics to multi-year highs while capex intensity continues to climb.

---

## Growth

Revenue expanded steadily from $572.8B (FY2022) to $713.2B (FY2026), a cumulative gain of 24.5% over four years. Annual growth rates accelerated from 2.4% (FY2022) to a window peak of 6.7% (FY2023), then decelerated gradually to 6.0% (FY2024), 5.1% (FY2025), and 4.7% (FY2026). The inflection point was FY2023: a surge in growth rate that coincided with the weakest profitability year in the dataset — a pairing examined further in Profitability. The deceleration since FY2023 is measured rather than abrupt, and the quarterly cadence in FY2026 suggests a partial re-acceleration: after a soft Q1 FY2026 (+2.5% YoY), growth firmed to +4.8% in Q2, +5.8% in Q3, and +5.6% in Q4 — all comparisons against the equivalent prior-year quarter.

Gross profit grew from $143.8B (FY2022) to $177.8B (FY2026), broadly in line with revenue, while operating income grew from $25.9B to $29.8B — a 15.0% gain over the same span, modestly slower than the 24.5% top-line gain, reflecting the margin dynamics detailed below. EPS (diluted) expanded far more rapidly, from $1.62 (FY2022) to $2.73 (FY2026), a 68.5% cumulative rise, amplified by both earnings growth and a steadily declining share count. The gap between revenue growth and EPS growth captures the operating leverage and capital-returns compounding at work over this period.

## Profitability

Revenue growth and operating margin tell a bifurcated story. Growth accelerated into FY2023, but operating margin simultaneously collapsed from 4.53% (FY2022) to 3.34% (FY2023) — the lowest annual reading in the dataset — while gross margin fell from 25.10% to 24.14%. FY2023 therefore represents a clear inflection: more revenue at meaningfully lower profitability per dollar. The data cannot identify the cause from these figures alone; the company's MD&A for FY2023 should be consulted. One possible driver — the data cannot confirm this — is elevated operating cost inflation and inventory adjustment charges in that year.

Recovery commenced in FY2024. Gross margin climbed back to 24.38%, and operating margin recovered to 4.17%. The trajectory extended through FY2025 (gross margin 24.85%, operating margin 4.31%) and held roughly flat in FY2026 (gross margin 24.93%, operating margin 4.18%). By FY2026, gross margin had nearly fully recovered toward FY2022 levels, but operating margin at 4.18% remained 35 basis points below the FY2022 peak of 4.53% — indicating that below-gross-profit cost lines have not fully reverted. The quarterly data corroborates the pattern: Q3 FY2023 operating margin fell to 1.76% — anomalously low — before normalizing through FY2024 onward.

Net margin tells a more favorable story than operating margin. Net margin in FY2026 (3.07%) is 68 basis points above FY2022 (2.39%), even though operating margin is 35 basis points lower. This divergence implies meaningful improvement in below-the-operating-line items: the effective tax rate fell from 32.89% (FY2023 peak) to 24.75% (FY2026), and other income/expense items appear to have shifted materially in WMT's favor between FY2024 and FY2026 — from an implied drag of approximately $5.9B in FY2024 to roughly $0.7B in FY2026. The data cannot decompose these line items; the consolidated income statement and MD&A would need to be reviewed to attribute the improvement.

## Cash Generation

Operating cash flow (OCF) grew from $24.2B (FY2022) to $41.6B (FY2026), a 71.9% cumulative increase substantially faster than either revenue (+24.5%) or net income (+60.1%). This outperformance of cash generation relative to accounting earnings is one of the most significant positive signals in the dataset. The OCF-to-Net-Income ratio — the primary earnings quality diagnostic — ranged between 1.77x (FY2022) and 2.47x (FY2023), settling at 1.90x in FY2026. A ratio consistently well above 1.0x indicates that reported net income is backed by, and exceeded by, cash from operations.

The FY2023 ratio of 2.47x warrants specific attention: OCF was $28.8B against net income of only $11.7B. The magnitude of the gap in a year when accounting earnings were depressed suggests either significant non-cash charges running through the income statement or favorable working capital movements (or both) in that year. The data cannot isolate these components; MD&A and the cash flow statement detail are required. The ratio's subsequent normalization to the 1.88x–1.90x range in FY2025–FY2026 — as both OCF and net income expanded — is consistent with the FY2023 gap being driven partly by one-time factors.

Free cash flow (FCF) presents the primary caveat to this otherwise strong cash-generation picture. While OCF surged, rapidly escalating capital expenditures absorbed an increasing share of that cash: capex rose from $13.1B (FY2022) to $26.6B (FY2026), doubling in four years, and capex as a percentage of revenue expanded from 2.29% to 3.74%. As a result, FCF grew only from $11.1B (FY2022) to $14.9B (FY2026) — a 34.6% cumulative gain compared to OCF's 71.9% — and FCF margin improved only modestly from 1.93% to 2.09%, with an intermediate dip to 1.86% in FY2025. Depreciation and amortization rising from $10.7B to $14.2B over the same period confirms the growing asset base. The capex trajectory raises an open question: what is the composition and expected return timeline of this investment? The data cannot answer this; the capital expenditure disclosures in the 10-K are the appropriate source.

## Financial Health & Solvency

Walmart carries a structurally sub-1.0x current ratio, reading 0.93x in FY2022 and edging down to 0.79x in FY2026. This pattern is common for large-scale retailers operating on a negative working capital model — suppliers are paid on extended terms while customers pay at point of sale — and should not be interpreted through the same lens applied to non-retail businesses. That said, the slow erosion from 0.93x to 0.79x over four years is a trend worth monitoring, particularly against the backdrop of rising capex commitments.

Leverage metrics improved materially over the window. Debt-to-Equity peaked at 0.51x in FY2023 and fell to 0.38x by FY2026. Net Debt peaked at $30.2B in FY2023 (when the cash balance fell sharply from $14.8B to $8.6B) and declined to $27.4B by FY2026. Net Debt to EBITDA peaked at 0.96x in FY2023 — approaching but not breaching 1.0x — and compressed to 0.62x in FY2026, a level consistent with a conservatively leveraged balance sheet for a company of Walmart's operating stability. Total equity grew from $83.3B (FY2022) to $99.6B (FY2026) despite simultaneous buybacks and dividends, reflecting the pace of earnings retention.

Interest coverage dipped from 15.5x (FY2022) to 11.4x (FY2023) — the weakest reading, coinciding with the operating income trough — and partially recovered to 12.9x by FY2026. At no point did coverage approach distress territory; even the FY2023 trough left significant debt-service headroom. Total debt was $37.7B in FY2022, edged up to $39.6B by FY2024, and declined to $38.2B in FY2026 — largely stable in absolute terms even as EBITDA and equity base expanded, which is the primary reason leverage ratios improved. The FY2023 inflection — when net debt, leverage ratios, and interest coverage all weakened simultaneously — represents the single sharpest stress point visible in this dataset.

## Capital Allocation

The share count declined from 8.415B diluted shares (FY2022) to 8.022B (FY2026), a reduction of 393M shares or approximately 4.7% over four years, averaging roughly 1.2% per annum. The trajectory was uneven: the fastest reduction occurred in FY2022–FY2023 (213M shares, roughly 2.5%), while FY2024 and FY2025 saw near-stagnation (only 27M shares combined), before modest resumption in FY2026 (59M shares). The buyback spending data mirrors this pattern: $9.8B (FY2022), $9.9B (FY2023), then a sharp contraction to $2.8B (FY2024), $4.5B (FY2025), and partial recovery to $8.1B (FY2026). The FY2024 pullback in buybacks directly coincided with the largest single-year capex step-up in the dataset (from $16.9B to $20.6B), suggesting that management prioritized reinvestment over returns to shareholders in that year. The data cannot confirm this was an explicit trade-off; MD&A commentary on capital allocation priorities would clarify.

Dividends followed a different pattern: essentially flat from FY2022 ($6.15B) through FY2024 ($6.14B), then stepped up to $6.69B (FY2025) and $7.51B (FY2026) — a 22% increase from the FY2024 level in just two years. The acceleration in dividends, combined with the resumption of buybacks, produced total FY2026 shareholder returns (buybacks + dividends) of approximately $15.6B, comparable to the FY2022–FY2023 peak of $15.9B–$16.0B after the sharp pullback in FY2024 ($8.9B). The capex commitment shows no sign of plateauing — rising from 2.29% to 3.74% of revenue over the window — so the sustainability of both dividend growth and buyback levels against a backdrop of increasing investment intensity is the central open question in capital allocation.

Return metrics strengthened considerably after the FY2023 trough. ROA improved from 4.79% (FY2023) to 8.03% (FY2026); ROE from 14.60% to 22.97%; ROIC from 11.60% to 17.80% — though ROIC slipped marginally to 16.95% in FY2026 after peaking in FY2025. The ROIC dip while ROA and ROE continued climbing is consistent with the expanding total invested capital base (driven by capex) not yet fully earning through to returns — a lag effect common in capital-intensive investment cycles. Whether the new capacity will ultimately sustain or improve ROIC cannot be determined from the financial data alone.

## Summary

Walmart's five-year financial trajectory is characterized by three phases: a compressed-margin, high-growth FY2023 that proved to be the nadir for profitability; a multi-year earnings recovery from FY2024 onward that pushed net income, EPS, ROA, and ROE to window highs; and a parallel capex surge that has continuously absorbed a larger share of operating cash flow, constraining FCF expansion even as OCF grew strongly. By FY2026, revenue stands at $713.2B (+24.5% since FY2022), net income at $21.9B (+60.1%), and EPS at $2.73 (+68.5%), with OCF of $41.6B substantially covering all capital deployment needs. The balance sheet deleveraged meaningfully after its FY2023 peak, with Net Debt to EBITDA at 0.62x — among the lowest readings in the window. The unresolved trajectory questions center on two related dynamics: whether operating margin can close the remaining 35 basis-point gap to its FY2022 peak of 4.53% as the cost base continues to expand, and whether the five-year capex ramp — now consuming 3.74% of revenue annually versus 2.29% in FY2022 — will translate into sufficient incremental operating returns to sustain ROIC at or above current levels.
